SESSION 42: KEY-PREDICTORS NOTE

Research Objective
The purpose of this analysis was to identify the predictors that contributed
most strongly to the Random Forest model's prediction of students' final
grade, G3.

Method
A RandomForestRegressor containing 300 trees was trained using the complete
early-prediction dataset. G3 was used as the target. G1 and G2 were excluded
from the predictors so earlier course grades would not dominate the
early-warning model. Categorical predictors were converted into numeric
indicator columns through one-hot encoding.

Top Ten Predictors
1. absences: 0.180569 (18.06%)
2. failures: 0.137361 (13.74%)
3. goout: 0.047060 (4.71%)
4. age: 0.037091 (3.71%)
5. freetime: 0.035805 (3.58%)
6. health: 0.035733 (3.57%)
7. Medu: 0.032359 (3.24%)
8. Walc: 0.032209 (3.22%)
9. studytime: 0.031190 (3.12%)
10. Fedu: 0.026043 (2.60%)

Summary
The highest-ranked predictor was absences, with an importance
of 0.180569 (18.06%).
The next two predictors were failures and
goout. The top three predictors accounted for
36.50% of total importance, the top five accounted for
43.79%, and the top ten accounted for 59.54%.

Comparison with Hypothesis H4
Hypothesis H4 proposed that academic, behavioral, demographic, family, and
school-related variables would not contribute equally to final-grade
prediction. These results partially supports H4 because the predictors have unequal importance, although the top five do not account for at least half of total importance.

Interpretation
The ranking identifies the predictors that the Random Forest used most
strongly when separating students with different final-grade outcomes.
Feature importance describes predictive association; it is not evidence of
causation. Correlated predictors may divide or redistribute importance between
themselves, while one-hot encoding may divide one categorical predictor's
importance across several indicator columns.

Recommendation
Use the importance ranking to identify predictors for further analysis, but do
not use it as the sole basis for educational intervention, labeling, punitive
action, or automatic decisions. Confirm the results with permutation
importance, correlation analysis, and subject-matter knowledge.
